# 1. Purpose

This document defines the canonical content object used by The Auric and the Named Terminal network.

The canonical content object is the durable, structured record behind every publishable or distributable intelligence product.

It is not merely an article body.

It must preserve:

- what the content is
- why it exists
- which entities it concerns
- which claims it makes
- what evidence supports those claims
- who created and reviewed it
- what conflicts or commercial relationships exist
- where it was distributed
- what profile and graph updates it caused
- what corrections, disputes, and versions followed
- what audience and institutional outcomes resulted

The purpose of the object is to ensure that publication, profiles, intelligence, distribution, and institutional action operate from one governed record rather than disconnected copies.

The core doctrine is:

**Every material Auric output should exist first as a canonical content object. Channel-specific articles, emails, social posts, alerts, Terminal briefs, board briefs, and scripts are governed derivatives of that object.**

# 2. Why a Canonical Content Object Is Required

Traditional publishing systems often treat content as a page or post.

That is insufficient for The Auric.

The Auric must support:

- evidence-backed reporting
- public profiles
- Named Terminal personalization
- structured market intelligence
- omnichannel distribution
- profile enrichment
- corrections
- conflict disclosures
- commercial separation
- audience measurement
- opportunity signals
- institutional search
- long-term market memory

Without a canonical object, the system would create fragmented records across:

- CMS articles
- email newsletters
- social posts
- video scripts
- Terminal feeds
- profile pages
- internal research notes
- corrections
- reports
- analytics systems

That fragmentation would weaken:

- source lineage
- consistency
- corrections
- profile accuracy
- editorial control
- distribution
- analytics
- institutional relevance
- auditability

The canonical content object becomes the common spine connecting editorial judgment to Dispatch-powered intelligence.

# 3. Content Object Classes

The system should support multiple content classes under one common structure.

## 3.1 Breaking Alert

Fast, concise, source-backed notice of a material development.

## 3.2 News Article

Straight reporting on an event, announcement, change, transaction, or institutional development.

## 3.3 Analysis

Interpretive content explaining context, implications, tradeoffs, and likely consequences.

## 3.4 Daily or Weekly Brief

Curated collection of relevant developments.

## 3.5 Profile Feature

Narrative and structured treatment of an institution, company, executive, board, CUSO, product, or category.

## 3.6 Regulatory Brief

Operational explanation of a rule, regulatory change, examination theme, enforcement action, or policy development.

## 3.7 Market Map

Structured presentation of a category, its participants, relationships, products, and positioning.

## 3.8 Ranking

Methodology-backed ordering or classification of market participants.

## 3.9 Data Story

Narrative built from structured data, comparative analysis, or longitudinal trends.

## 3.10 Interview

Conversation with an executive, founder, investor, regulator, operator, or subject-matter expert.

## 3.11 Opinion

Clearly labeled viewpoint.

## 3.12 Sponsored Content

Commercially supported content that remains visibly distinct from independent reporting.

## 3.13 Company-Submitted Update

Verified company-supplied information.

## 3.14 Terminal Brief

Private or permissioned content tailored to a Named Terminal, institution, persona, committee, or workflow.

## 3.15 Board or Executive Brief

Structured decision-oriented content intended for executive or board consumption.

Each class shares the same governance foundation but may use different required fields, review standards, channel rules, and approval requirements.

# 5. Content State Model

The content object should move through explicit states.

## 5.1 Signal

A potential story or intelligence item exists.

At this stage:

- the subject may be unresolved
- evidence may be incomplete
- no publication commitment exists

## 5.2 Research Open

The item has:

- assigned owner
- working premise
- identified entities
- initial source plan
- urgency
- target audience

## 5.3 Evidence Collection

Sources, documents, interviews, and data are being assembled.

## 5.4 Draft

A working editorial artifact exists.

## 5.5 Fact Review

Material claims are being checked against evidence.

## 5.6 Editorial Review

The content is being reviewed for relevance, framing, balance, completeness, tone, and quality.

## 5.7 Conflict Review

Commercial, personal, investment, partner, or customer conflicts are being evaluated.

## 5.8 Legal or Compliance Review

Required where risk thresholds are met.

## 5.9 Approved

The canonical content object is approved for specified distribution.

## 5.10 Scheduled

Publication or delivery is scheduled.

## 5.11 Published

The canonical object has at least one active public or private distribution.

## 5.12 Updated

New information has changed the content without invalidating the prior record.

## 5.13 Corrected

A material or nonmaterial error has been corrected.

## 5.14 Disputed

A subject or source has challenged one or more claims.

## 5.15 Superseded

A newer canonical object replaces the current object for ongoing use while preserving history.

## 5.16 Retracted

The content is withdrawn because it cannot be supported or because publication materially failed.

## 5.17 Archived

The content remains part of the historical record but is not actively distributed.

---

# 6. State Transition Rules

State transitions must be governed.

Examples:

- Signal may move to Research Open only when an owner and premise exist.
- Research Open may move to Draft only when minimum evidence exists.
- Draft may not move to Approved while material claims lack source support.
- Conflict Review is mandatory where a disclosed commercial relationship exists.
- Legal Review is mandatory for allegations, litigation, enforcement, or high reputational risk.
- Sponsored Content cannot enter Published state without visible sponsorship labeling.
- Company-Submitted Update cannot be presented as independent reporting.
- Retracted content must preserve the original record and reason.
- Corrected content must preserve prior text or a record of the corrected claim.
- Archived content may still appear in timelines and historical profiles.

No agent may independently move material content into Approved, Published, Retracted, or dispute-resolved state.

# 7. Claim Classification

Claims should be classified so readers and systems understand their nature.

## 7.1 Verified Fact

Supported by authoritative evidence.

## 7.2 Reported Fact

Supported by credible sources but not necessarily official confirmation.

## 7.3 Subject Statement

Attributed statement from the subject.

## 7.4 Analysis

Interpretation based on facts and context.

## 7.5 Estimate

Calculated or modeled value with disclosed assumptions.

## 7.6 Prediction

Forward-looking judgment.

## 7.7 Opinion

Clearly attributed viewpoint.

## 7.8 Allegation

Claim of wrongdoing or disputed conduct requiring elevated review.

## 7.9 Company-Submitted Claim

Information supplied by a company or institution and labeled accordingly.

## 7.10 Inference

System or analyst conclusion derived from multiple inputs.

Inference must never silently become verified fact.

The lifecycle should recognize specific roles.

## 9.1 Reporter or Analyst

May:

- open research
- collect evidence
- draft
- propose claims
- propose profile updates

May not:

- approve high-risk content
- resolve conflicts alone
- publish material allegations independently

## 9.2 Editor

May:

- assign
- frame
- revise
- approve ordinary content within authority
- determine format and placement

## 9.3 Data Editor

May:

- validate datasets
- review calculations
- approve charts
- assess methodology

## 9.4 Profile Editor

May:

- review profile update proposals
- distinguish public, claimed, and inferred fields
- resolve ordinary profile disputes

## 9.5 Conflict Reviewer

May:

- assess commercial or personal conflicts
- require disclosure
- escalate
- restrict publication treatment

## 9.6 Legal or Compliance Reviewer

May:

- assess legal, regulatory, privilege, and reputational risk
- impose conditions
- block publication within authority

## 9.7 Editor in Chief or Designated Authority

May:

- approve high-risk publication
- approve retraction
- resolve major editorial disputes
- approve methodology for material rankings

## 9.8 Agents

May:

- retrieve
- extract
- summarize
- compare
- draft
- classify
- route
- monitor
- generate derivatives

May not:

- approve publication
- resolve material disputes
- decide legal sufficiency
- remove disclosures
- fabricate sources
- rewrite public history without review

# 13. Dispute Lifecycle

A dispute should move through:

1. Submitted
2. Identity verified
3. Claim identified
4. Evidence requested
5. Under review
6. Subject response recorded
7. Editorial decision
8. Correction, clarification, denial, or no change
9. Appeal if permitted
10. Closed

The dispute process should preserve:

- claimant identity
- relationship to subject
- claim challenged
- evidence submitted
- reviewer
- decision
- rationale
- publication action
- profile action

A dispute is not automatically evidence that the content is wrong.

A commercial customer must not receive a privileged correction standard unavailable to others.

# 24. Controlling Statement

The canonical content object is the durable record behind The Auric.

It connects editorial judgment to source evidence, public profiles, Named Terminal intelligence, distribution, corrections, relationships, and institutional action.

Articles, emails, social posts, alerts, scripts, and briefs are not independent truths.

They are governed expressions of one canonical record.

The system wins when every piece of content preserves what is known, what is claimed, what is inferred, who approved it, where it traveled, what it changed, and what institutional value it created.

## 25.9 Content-to-Profile Mapping Rules

1. Publication does not directly mutate a canonical profile.
2. Publication creates a `ProfileUpdateProposal` containing entity, field, proposed value, source IDs, confidence, effective date, and provenance class.
3. Public profile proposals require profile-governance review.
4. Private Terminal profile proposals remain tenant-scoped unless explicitly authorized for public use.
5. Inferred facts remain labeled inferred after approval; approval does not convert inference into attestation.
6. Stale values remain visible until superseded or explicitly retired.
7. Conflicting values create a reconciliation case rather than silent overwrite.
8. Corrections to content automatically reopen affected profile proposals.

## 25.17 Editorial Operations Runbook

### Daily operation

1. Review incoming assignments, alerts, source changes, disputes, and correction queues.
2. Assign content owner, desk, urgency, and required review path.
3. Confirm source classification and source-protection requirements.
4. Validate material claims before editorial approval.
5. Review conflict and commercial disclosures.
6. Approve, schedule, and publish through authorized human roles.
7. Monitor derivatives, correction propagation, and profile-update proposals.
8. Close the day with exception, stale-source, and unresolved-dispute review.

### Publication incident

1. Suspend affected content and derivatives when material risk exists.
2. Preserve all versions and audit evidence.
3. Notify managing editor, legal/compliance, security, and affected tenant owner as applicable.
4. Determine correction, retraction, or restoration path.
5. Regenerate derivatives and reopen profile proposals.
6. Record closure evidence and post-incident control improvements.

## 25.18 Correction and Retraction Runbook

1. Open correction or retraction case.
2. Identify affected claims, derivatives, profiles, alerts, and reports.
3. Assess severity: clerical, substantive, material, or legal/safety critical.
4. Collect replacement evidence and subject response where appropriate.
5. Obtain required human approvals.
6. Create immutable corrected version or retraction notice.
7. Propagate to all channels.
8. Confirm public notice visibility.
9. Reconcile profile and graph effects.
10. Close only when propagation evidence passes.

## 25.19 Migration Guidance

Legacy articles and channel posts should be migrated in this order:

1. assign canonical content ID
2. ingest original body and metadata
3. identify entities and claims
4. attach preserved sources where available
5. classify unknown, stale, inferred, and unsupported claims explicitly
6. link derivatives
7. preserve original publication timestamps
8. create correction history where discoverable
9. prohibit automatic profile mutation from low-confidence legacy material
10. mark migration confidence and review status
