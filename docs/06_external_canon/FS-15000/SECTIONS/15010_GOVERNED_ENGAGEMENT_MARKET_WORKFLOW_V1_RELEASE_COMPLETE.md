# FS-15010 — Governed Engagement & Market Workflow
## Converting Institutional Relevance into Permissioned, Evidence-Backed Action

**Section:** FS-15000 — The Auric, Named Terminal & Institutional Network Flywheel  
**Document:** 15010  
**Status:** V1 Release Complete
**Authority:** Controlling doctrine for all governed engagements across institutions, fintechs, CUSOs, vendors, partners, investors, and network participants  
**Applies to:** Introductions, inquiries, information requests, demonstrations, diligence, pilots, procurement, partnerships, referrals, investments, loan participations, implementations, renewals, expansions, evidence rooms, approvals, and outcomes

---

# 1. Purpose

This document defines how market interest becomes governed institutional action.

The Named Terminal network may produce:

- profile views
- follows
- relationship signals
- content engagement
- company discovery
- opportunity detection
- institutional matching
- introductions
- requests for information
- pilot interest
- investment interest
- partnership interest
- vendor-review activity

Those signals are not themselves institutional decisions.

When activity moves toward material engagement, the system must convert the signal into a governed workflow with:

- identified parties
- verified identity
- explicit purpose
- relationship ownership
- permissions
- evidence
- readiness
- approval authority
- workflow state
- obligations
- deliverables
- commercial context
- audit history
- measurable outcome

The governing doctrine is:

**The network may create relevance.  
The Named Terminal must convert relevance into governed engagement before institutional action occurs.  
Dispatch OS must absorb workflow complexity while exposing evidence, authority, risk, approval, and outcome.**

---

# 2. Why Governed Engagement Is Required

A financial-services network cannot operate like a generic social or professional network.

Institutional activity may involve:

- regulated data
- confidential information
- procurement
- vendor oversight
- credit
- investments
- referrals
- insurance
- securities
- board authority
- legal obligations
- member or customer impact
- material reputation risk

An informal interaction may be acceptable for:

- following
- reading
- commenting
- requesting a public connection
- attending an event
- viewing a profile

A governed workflow becomes necessary when the parties begin to:

- exchange diligence
- share confidential evidence
- evaluate a product
- discuss an institution-specific use case
- request pricing
- design a pilot
- make a referral
- allocate capital
- enter procurement
- approve implementation
- execute a transaction

Without a governed engagement model, the network risks becoming:

- a cold-lead marketplace
- an uncontrolled data room
- a pay-to-play introduction channel
- a relationship-driven bypass of institutional governance
- an opaque recommendation engine
- a compliance liability

# 3. Engagement Classes

The system should support distinct engagement classes.

## 3.1 Informational Inquiry

Purpose:

- ask a question
- request public information
- seek clarification
- identify the correct contact

Generally low risk.

## 3.2 Controlled Introduction

Purpose:

- connect two verified parties around a stated institutional purpose

Requires:

- consent
- context
- owner
- next step
- visibility rules

## 3.3 Information Request

Purpose:

- request product, company, institution, evidence, or market information

May be public, private, or diligence-oriented.

## 3.4 Demonstration

Purpose:

- show product or capability

Should identify:

- audience
- use case
- data restrictions
- no-production status
- follow-up owner

## 3.5 Diligence

Purpose:

- evaluate company, product, institution, investment, vendor, or transaction

Requires structured evidence and review.

## 3.6 Pilot

Purpose:

- test a defined capability under controlled conditions

Requires measurable scope and approval.

## 3.7 Procurement or Vendor Selection

Purpose:

- evaluate and select a provider

Requires institutional authority, evidence, and decision record.

## 3.8 Partnership

Purpose:

- establish a commercial, strategic, distribution, implementation, or operating relationship

## 3.9 Referral

Purpose:

- route a qualified opportunity to a permitted provider or partner

May require:

- consent
- licensing
- disclosure
- attribution
- compensation rules

## 3.10 Investment or Capital Engagement

Purpose:

- evaluate or execute investment, CUSO participation, fund participation, private credit, or related capital activity

Requires heightened legal, compliance, authority, and suitability controls.

## 3.11 Loan Participation or Transaction Engagement

Purpose:

- originate, evaluate, allocate, purchase, sell, settle, or monitor an institutional financial transaction

## 3.12 Implementation

Purpose:

- deploy an approved product, service, workflow, or partnership

## 3.13 Renewal or Expansion

Purpose:

- continue, extend, deepen, or broaden an existing relationship

## 3.14 Remediation or Exit

Purpose:

- correct failure
- terminate relationship
- transition service
- preserve records
- manage obligations

# 6. Signal Versus Engagement

A signal may include:

- profile view
- content interaction
- relationship path
- opportunity match
- institution interest
- company interest
- partner recommendation
- board priority
- contract renewal
- regulatory requirement
- readiness improvement

A signal should not automatically create an active engagement.

The system should first determine:

- Is the interest real?
- Is the purpose clear?
- Are the parties eligible?
- Does consent exist?
- Is there an owner?
- Is the engagement permitted?
- Are conflicts visible?
- Is readiness sufficient?
- Is evidence required before opening?

Only then should the system create or activate a governed engagement.

The Named Terminal network may create discovery, relevance, and relationships.

Institutional action begins only when that activity becomes a governed engagement.

# Volume-Specific Implementation Artifacts


## Engagement Object Schema

Required fields: engagement ID, class, purpose, originating signal, parties, institutional sponsor, operational owner, decision authorities, tenant scopes, visibility, consent records, readiness assessments, evidence requirements, approval requirements, commercial relationships, conflicts, milestones, communications, tasks, state, outcome, closure reason, reputation-update eligibility, and audit references.

## Engagement Classes

The canonical classes are diligence, pilot, procurement, partnership, referral, investment, implementation, renewal, expansion, remediation, and strategic collaboration. Each class has an independent evidence and approval profile and may not be silently converted into another class.

## State Machine

`draft → proposed → consent_pending → qualification → evidence_collection → diligence → approval_pending → approved → active → measurement → closure_pending → completed`.

Alternate terminal states are `declined`, `withdrawn`, `suspended`, `failed`, `terminated`, and `expired`. Reopening requires a new version or successor engagement with preserved lineage.

## Sponsor / Owner / Authority Matrix

The sponsor advocates for institutional relevance. The owner runs the work. The approval authority decides the material action. The compliance, security, legal, risk, procurement, investment, or credit authority decides only within delegated scope. Auric Works may facilitate but may not substitute for institutional authority.

## Evidence and Approval Matrix

Diligence requires identity, capability, security, compliance, financial, operational, and reference evidence as applicable. Pilots add scope, success metrics, data flows, exit criteria, and sponsor approval. Procurement adds commercial, legal, vendor-risk, and budget approvals. Investments add suitability, authority, diligence, conflicts, valuation, and investment-committee approval. Implementations add production architecture, security, change, training, support, rollback, and go-live approval.

## APIs and Events

Core endpoints cover engagement creation, consent, evidence requests, readiness, approval submission, milestone updates, communication capture, suspension, closure, dispute, and export. Events include `engagement.created`, `consent.recorded`, `readiness.completed`, `evidence.accepted`, `approval.requested`, `approval.decided`, `engagement.activated`, `milestone.completed`, `engagement.suspended`, `outcome.recorded`, and `engagement.closed`.

## Synthetic Fixture

A fintech pilot fixture includes one credit union, one fintech, a named executive sponsor, an operational owner, security and compliance reviewers, defined data categories, required evidence, a 90-day scope, measurable success criteria, explicit human approvals, commercial disclosures, and a closure record. The fixture must prove that a match does not equal selection and an agent recommendation does not equal approval.

## Dispute and Escalation Runbook

Any party may dispute scope, evidence, authority, commercial treatment, outcome, or reputation effect. The system freezes the disputed downstream update, preserves the record, assigns an independent owner, sets severity and SLA, obtains evidence, records the resolution, and replays only authorized downstream effects.

# Production Implementation Specification

## Canonical Record Requirements

Every object defined by this volume SHALL include a stable identifier, tenant or public-scope identifier, version, lifecycle state, provenance class, source references, confidence, freshness, owner, created and updated timestamps, review state, visibility policy, retention policy, and audit history. Public, private, synthetic, attested, inferred, and agent-generated records SHALL remain distinguishable in storage, retrieval, display, export, and downstream decision support.

Unknown, stale, disputed, incomplete, and unavailable values SHALL be represented explicitly. No implementation may coerce missing values into false, zero, approved, verified, or current values.

## Permission and Authority Controls

Roles are evaluated through FS-6000 authority records and FS-12000 IAM policy. Agents may retrieve, classify, compare, summarize, calculate, draft, recommend, route, monitor, and assemble evidence. Agents may not approve regulated or material actions, commit capital, bind an institution, publish a material disputed assertion without editorial approval, suppress a correction, waive policy, or disclose private tenant information.

Material actions require an Approval Object that records the action, evidence set, requested authority, approver identity, decision, conditions, timestamp, expiration, and audit reference.

## API Conventions

All APIs use versioned resource paths, idempotency keys for material writes, optimistic concurrency through record version or ETag, tenant and actor context, correlation IDs, explicit error objects, and audit-event emission. Typical operations include create, retrieve, list, update, submit, approve, reject, suspend, resume, close, dispute, correct, and export where permitted.

Every material mutation emits an event containing event ID, event type, aggregate ID, aggregate version, tenant or public scope, actor, causation ID, correlation ID, occurred-at timestamp, provenance class, and redacted payload appropriate to the subscriber.

## Validation Rules

1. IDs are unique within their canonical namespace.
2. State transitions must appear in the approved state machine.
3. Required evidence must be current and permitted for the stated use.
4. Approval authority must match the requested material action.
5. Public and private fields may not be merged into an indistinguishable record.
6. Attested claims do not become verified facts without the applicable verification rule.
7. Inferred or agent-generated conclusions must include method, confidence, and review state.
8. Stale records remain visible as stale until refreshed or retired.
9. Disputed records retain the original, dispute, resolution, and version history.
10. Commercial relationships and material conflicts must be disclosed where relevant.

## Observability and Analytics

The implementation SHALL record volume, state conversion, cycle time, error rate, approval latency, evidence completeness, freshness, dispute rate, correction rate, abandonment, successful outcome, and downstream reuse. Metrics must be segmented by tenant, role, object class, workflow class, source class, and channel only where privacy and contractual rules permit.

## Failure Handling

Failures are classified as validation, permission, evidence, dependency, integration, policy, conflict, timeout, duplicate, stale-data, or internal errors. Recoverable failures may retry under bounded policy. Material or repeated failures create an exception record, preserve evidence, notify the accountable owner, and route to human review. Compensation actions must never erase audit history.

## Acceptance Test Catalog

- create a valid canonical record and retrieve its complete lineage;
- reject a duplicate canonical ID;
- reject an invalid state transition;
- preserve unknown and stale values explicitly;
- prevent private fields from entering public output;
- prevent an agent from exercising material approval authority;
- require current evidence before a material action;
- record a human approval with conditions and expiration;
- disclose a material commercial conflict;
- support dispute, correction, versioning, and audit retrieval;
- emit the required events for every material transition;
- preserve tenant isolation in search, export, graph traversal, and agent context.

## Operations Runbook

The accountable product owner maintains registries, state machines, decision tables, permissions, templates, and metrics. Operations reviews failed and stalled records daily, stale or expiring evidence on the configured cadence, disputes and corrections within policy SLA, and material conflict or security events immediately. Changes require versioning, impact analysis, test evidence, approval, release notes, and rollback guidance.

## Migration Guidance

Baseline records are migrated by assigning canonical IDs, provenance classes, lifecycle states, visibility, source links, confidence, freshness, and owners. Ambiguous records enter a review queue. Legacy names are retained as aliases rather than silently rewritten. No record becomes verified, approved, or public merely because it existed in a prior system.

## Release Validation

The volume is content-complete when narrative doctrine, canonical objects, schemas, registries, state models, decision tables, permissions, evidence, approvals, APIs, events, fixtures, failure behavior, tests, runbooks, cross-section references, release manifest entries, and integrity records are present and internally consistent. Documentation completion does not certify software implementation or production readiness.
