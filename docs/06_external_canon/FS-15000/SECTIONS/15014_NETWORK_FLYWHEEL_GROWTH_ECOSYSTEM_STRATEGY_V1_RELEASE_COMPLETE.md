# FS-15014 — Network Flywheel, Growth & Ecosystem Strategy
## Compounding Institutional Intelligence Through Publication, Identity, Readiness, Relationships, Execution, and Verified Outcomes

**Version:** 1.0  
**Status:** V1 Release Complete  
**Authority:** Controlling network-growth and ecosystem specification  
**Applies to:** The Auric, Named Terminals, Dispatch OS, public and claimed profiles, readiness, matching, governed engagement, implementation, verified outcomes, relationships, distribution, and ecosystem expansion

---

# 1. Purpose

This volume defines how the Dispatch / Auric market-facing system compounds without becoming a generic media audience, social network, lead marketplace, or pay-to-play vendor directory.

The canonical flywheel is:

**Content → Discovery → Profiles → Named Terminal → Readiness → Matching → Governed Engagement → Implementation → Verified Outcomes → Better Content.**

Growth is valid only when it improves institutional intelligence, profile quality, governed execution, or verified outcomes. Vanity traffic, purchased ranking, unqualified introductions, and opaque commercial preference do not constitute network value.

# 2. Network Thesis

The Auric creates legibility and direct audience. Public profiles turn market understanding into structured identity. Claimed and verified profiles improve completeness and accountability. Named Terminals provide private institutional utility. Readiness converts interest into evidence-backed capability. Matching identifies permitted fit. Governed engagement preserves consent, authority, evidence, and audit. Implementation and verified outcomes create durable reputation and better future intelligence.

The network is therefore not the starting product. It is the compounding result of operational value.

# 3. Primary Flywheel Loops

## 3.1 Content Loop

Signals become source-backed content; content enriches profiles; distribution creates reader and relationship signals; corrections and new evidence improve the public record.

## 3.2 Company Loop

A company is discovered, profiled, claimed, verified, assessed for readiness, matched to relevant institutions, engaged through controlled workflows, and improved through verified implementation outcomes.

## 3.3 Institution Loop

An institution receives relevant intelligence, completes its private profile, establishes priorities and readiness, evaluates opportunities, runs governed engagements, and preserves institutional learning.

## 3.4 Relationship Loop

Source-backed relationships create context and paths. Accepted interactions strengthen relationship evidence. Declines, conflicts, and outcomes update future relevance without exposing private context.

## 3.5 Evidence Loop

Evidence supplied once may be reused only within permission, freshness, and purpose limits. Reusable evidence reduces diligence time and improves future readiness.

## 3.6 Outcome Loop

Verified outcomes improve reputation, ranking inputs, editorial understanding, implementation playbooks, and future matching. Claimed outcomes remain attested until verified.

# 4. Canonical Growth Objects

The network-growth model uses: audience signal, content object, profile, profile claim, verification, readiness assessment, opportunity, match, relationship path, engagement, implementation, outcome, reference, reputation signal, ecosystem segment, campaign, channel, cohort, invitation, conversion, retention event, expansion event, and network contribution.

# 5. Flywheel State Model

`signal_detected → content_or_profile_created → distributed → discovered → profile_engaged → profile_claimed → private_context_added → readiness_completed → opportunity_eligible → match_generated → engagement_accepted → implementation_active → outcome_measured → outcome_verified → reputation_updated → intelligence_reused`.

A participant may exit, decline, pause, dispute, or remain unknown at any stage. The system does not fabricate forward progress.

# 6. Growth Eligibility Rules

A growth action is eligible when it has a legitimate audience or institutional purpose, permitted data, explainable targeting, accurate disclosures, and a measurable value hypothesis. It is ineligible when it depends on undisclosed payment, prohibited data, deceptive urgency, purchased verification, unauthorized private context, or bypass of institutional authority.

# 7. Distribution Strategy

The Auric may distribute through web, email, newsletters, direct briefings, feeds, partner channels, social channels, events, and Named Terminal surfaces. Every channel preserves canonical content identity, version, source, correction status, sponsorship label, and analytics consent. Channel optimization may alter format but not factual meaning.

# 8. Ecosystem Segmentation

Initial priority is credit unions, CUSOs, corporate credit unions, fintechs, vendors, regulators, leagues, trade associations, and aligned capital or implementation partners. Expansion to community banks and adjacent financial-services institutions occurs through compatible domain cartridges and evidence that the existing network creates value without degrading trust.

Vertical expansion outside financial services requires a Dispatch kernel and domain operating framework appropriate to that industry. A large audience alone does not justify expansion.

# 9. Growth Scoring Model

Network growth is evaluated through five dimensions:

1. **Intelligence contribution** — does the activity improve source-backed market understanding?
2. **Profile contribution** — does it improve identity, completeness, freshness, or verification?
3. **Institutional utility** — does it produce useful private work, readiness, or decisions?
4. **Execution contribution** — does it support a governed engagement or measurable implementation?
5. **Trust contribution** — does it improve transparency, outcomes, correction, and reputation?

Scores are confidence-weighted and may not treat raw engagement volume as equivalent to verified value.

# 10. KPI Registry

Core KPIs include direct audience, qualified readership, content-to-profile enrichment, profile claim rate, verification completion, Terminal activation, readiness completion, eligible opportunity rate, match acceptance, engagement conversion, evidence reuse, pilot conversion, implementation completion, verified outcome rate, retention, expansion, correction rate, dispute rate, time to value, diligence-cycle reduction, relationship-path quality, and network contribution per active participant.

# 11. Cohorts and Attribution

Attribution distinguishes editorial discovery, organic search, direct relationship, partner referral, event, campaign, Terminal recommendation, and institutional invitation. Multi-touch attribution must remain explainable and cannot overwrite the actual relationship path. Commercial attribution is separate from editorial relevance.

# 12. Invitations and Consent

Invitations identify sender, purpose, requested action, data use, expiration, and unsubscribe or decline options. Institutional invitations may not imply endorsement or approval. A public profile can exist without consent when lawfully sourced; private engagement requires applicable consent and authority.

# 13. Network Effects and Privacy

Shared infrastructure may improve from public information, consented contributions, and appropriately governed aggregate learning. Private tenant context cannot be exposed to create artificial network value. Similarity, ranking, and recommendations must enforce tenancy, field-level visibility, and model-use policy.

# 14. Reputation Model

Reputation is multidimensional: identity confidence, evidence completeness, readiness, implementation history, verified outcomes, responsiveness, dispute history, correction behavior, and relationship references. Reputation is not a single universal score and is not for sale. Negative or disputed outcomes require context, evidence, review, and appeal.

# 15. Growth Experiments

Every experiment defines hypothesis, eligible cohort, intervention, control or comparison, success metric, guardrails, duration, owner, approval, data classification, and stop conditions. Experiments involving ranking, matching, regulated decisions, private information, or material commercial treatment require governance review.

# 16. Commercial Growth Boundaries

Revenue may arise from platform fees, implementation, managed service, usage, pass-through costs, sponsorship, advertising, subscriptions, partnerships, and permitted transaction or execution activity. Commercial value may accelerate service, configuration, or support; it may not purchase editorial truth, verification, eligibility, hidden ranking, or institutional approval.

# 17. Partner and Channel Model

Partners may contribute audience, data, expertise, implementation, distribution, or relationships. Every partner record includes role, permissions, commercial terms, conflicts, data rights, service obligations, quality metrics, and termination rules. A partner cannot access private network context merely because it distributes The Auric or resells a Terminal.

# 18. Anti-Patterns

The system must not optimize for spam, list growth without relevance, unqualified lead volume, opaque ranking, manufactured scarcity, hidden referral economics, profile scraping without provenance, forced claims, private-data leakage, engagement without consent, or transaction volume detached from institutional value.

# 19. Network Operations

Operations maintains content calendars, profile queues, claim and verification queues, readiness completion, invitation quality, match review, engagement conversion, outcome verification, disputes, corrections, partner performance, and ecosystem coverage. Weekly reviews focus on stalled states and data quality. Monthly reviews evaluate cohorts and flywheel conversion. Quarterly reviews assess trust, ecosystem gaps, expansion, and constitutional compliance.

# 20. API and Event Contracts

APIs expose authorized operations for signals, content attribution, profile claims, invitations, readiness, matches, engagement conversion, outcomes, reputation, cohorts, and analytics. Canonical events include `network.signal.detected`, `content.distributed`, `profile.discovered`, `profile.claimed`, `terminal.activated`, `readiness.completed`, `match.generated`, `invitation.accepted`, `engagement.created`, `implementation.completed`, `outcome.verified`, `reputation.updated`, and `network.contribution.recorded`.

# 21. Synthetic End-to-End Fixture

A fixture begins with a public regulatory or market signal, produces an Auric brief, enriches a fintech and credit-union profile, triggers a profile claim, provisions private Terminal context, completes bilateral readiness, produces an explainable match, records consent, runs a governed pilot, measures an approved success metric, verifies the outcome, updates reputation, and creates a source-backed follow-up article. The fixture proves that no private data is published and no paid relationship changes verification or matching without disclosure.

# 22. Acceptance Criteria

The volume is satisfied when the six primary loops are measurable; every growth state preserves source, permission, and audit; content improves profiles; profiles improve private utility; readiness gates matching; matching does not equal selection; engagement requires consent and authority; outcomes require verification; reputation is explainable; commercial influence is disclosed; tenant privacy is preserved; expansion follows domain readiness; and every KPI distinguishes volume from institutional value.

# 24. Controlling Statement

The network grows when each participant receives real intelligence or operating value and each verified outcome makes the next institutional decision better. Growth that weakens truth, privacy, authority, or trust is not growth.

# Production Implementation Specification

## Canonical Record Requirements

Every object defined by this volume SHALL include a stable identifier, tenant or public-scope identifier, version, lifecycle state, provenance class, source references, confidence, freshness, owner, created and updated timestamps, review state, visibility policy, retention policy, and audit history. Public, private, synthetic, attested, inferred, and agent-generated records SHALL remain distinguishable in storage, retrieval, display, export, and downstream decision support.

Unknown, stale, disputed, incomplete, and unavailable values SHALL be represented explicitly. No implementation may coerce missing values into false, zero, approved, verified, or current values.

## Permission and Authority Controls

Roles are evaluated through FS-6000 authority records and FS-12000 IAM policy. Agents may retrieve, classify, compare, summarize, calculate, draft, recommend, route, monitor, and assemble evidence. Agents may not approve regulated or material actions, commit capital, bind an institution, publish a material disputed assertion without editorial approval, suppress a correction, waive policy, or disclose private tenant information.

Material actions require an Approval Object that records the action, evidence set, requested authority, approver identity, decision, conditions, timestamp, expiration, and audit reference.

## API Conventions

All APIs use versioned resource paths, idempotency keys for material writes, optimistic concurrency through record version or ETag, tenant and actor context, correlation IDs, explicit error objects, and audit-event emission. Typical operations include create, retrieve, list, update, submit, approve, reject, suspend, resume, close, dispute, correct, and export where permitted.

Every material mutation emits an event containing event ID, event type, aggregate ID, aggregate version, tenant or public scope, actor, causation ID, correlation ID, occurred-at timestamp, provenance class, and redacted payload appropriate to the subscriber.

## Validation Rules

1. IDs are unique within their canonical namespace.
2. State transitions must appear in the approved state machine.
3. Required evidence must be current and permitted for the stated use.
4. Approval authority must match the requested material action.
5. Public and private fields may not be merged into an indistinguishable record.
6. Attested claims do not become verified facts without the applicable verification rule.
7. Inferred or agent-generated conclusions must include method, confidence, and review state.
8. Stale records remain visible as stale until refreshed or retired.
9. Disputed records retain the original, dispute, resolution, and version history.
10. Commercial relationships and material conflicts must be disclosed where relevant.

## Observability and Analytics

The implementation SHALL record volume, state conversion, cycle time, error rate, approval latency, evidence completeness, freshness, dispute rate, correction rate, abandonment, successful outcome, and downstream reuse. Metrics must be segmented by tenant, role, object class, workflow class, source class, and channel only where privacy and contractual rules permit.

## Failure Handling

Failures are classified as validation, permission, evidence, dependency, integration, policy, conflict, timeout, duplicate, stale-data, or internal errors. Recoverable failures may retry under bounded policy. Material or repeated failures create an exception record, preserve evidence, notify the accountable owner, and route to human review. Compensation actions must never erase audit history.

## Acceptance Test Catalog

- create a valid canonical record and retrieve its complete lineage;
- reject a duplicate canonical ID;
- reject an invalid state transition;
- preserve unknown and stale values explicitly;
- prevent private fields from entering public output;
- prevent an agent from exercising material approval authority;
- require current evidence before a material action;
- record a human approval with conditions and expiration;
- disclose a material commercial conflict;
- support dispute, correction, versioning, and audit retrieval;
- emit the required events for every material transition;
- preserve tenant isolation in search, export, graph traversal, and agent context.

## Operations Runbook

The accountable product owner maintains registries, state machines, decision tables, permissions, templates, and metrics. Operations reviews failed and stalled records daily, stale or expiring evidence on the configured cadence, disputes and corrections within policy SLA, and material conflict or security events immediately. Changes require versioning, impact analysis, test evidence, approval, release notes, and rollback guidance.

## Migration Guidance

Baseline records are migrated by assigning canonical IDs, provenance classes, lifecycle states, visibility, source links, confidence, freshness, and owners. Ambiguous records enter a review queue. Legacy names are retained as aliases rather than silently rewritten. No record becomes verified, approved, or public merely because it existed in a prior system.

## Release Validation

The volume is content-complete when narrative doctrine, canonical objects, schemas, registries, state models, decision tables, permissions, evidence, approvals, APIs, events, fixtures, failure behavior, tests, runbooks, cross-section references, release manifest entries, and integrity records are present and internally consistent. Documentation completion does not certify software implementation or production readiness.
